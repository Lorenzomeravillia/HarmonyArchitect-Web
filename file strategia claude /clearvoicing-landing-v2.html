The problem

Why chords sound like a wall of sound.

Traditional ear training plays all notes on the same instrument. Your brain hears one mass of sound, not individual voices. That's not a talent issue — it's an acoustics issue.

🎹

Same timbre = same blob

When four notes share the same piano sound, your auditory cortex groups them into one stream. You hear "a chord" but can't pick out the third from the fifth. It all fuses together.

🧩

Too much at once

Hearing 4 notes, analyzing intervals, identifying chord quality, and determining voicing — all in one instant. That's 4 cognitive tasks competing for the same limited mental bandwidth.

⏱️

Pressure kills listening

Timers and score penalties shift your brain from analytical mode to survival mode. Under pressure, you stop listening carefully and start guessing. That's not training — it's panic.

How it works

The orchestra principle.

You can follow a cello in an orchestra even when 80 instruments play at once. ClearVoicing applies the same principle — timbral separation — to every chord you study.

1

Each voice gets its own instrument

Bass plays on double bass. Middle voices on trumpet and horn. Top voice on flute. Your brain naturally separates different timbres — it's the same reason you hear the singer over the band.

2

Colors show you the structure

Root, third, fifth, seventh — each harmonic function has a consistent color on the staff. See the structure while you hear it. Two channels of information reinforce each other.

3

Follow voices, not blocks

Instead of guessing a chord label, you learn how each voice moves to the next chord. This is voice leading — the way arrangers and composers actually think about harmony.

4

Listen as many times as you need

No countdown. No score penalty. No judgment. Replay is free, always. The best learning happens when you're calm and curious, not rushed and anxious.

Why it works

Not a gimmick.
Auditory science at work.

Every design decision is grounded in research on how the brain processes complex sound.

Timbral Stream Segregation

When frequencies share the same timbre, the auditory cortex groups them as one stream. Different timbres create separate perceptual streams. This is why you can follow a cello in an orchestra.

Dual Coding

Information encoded through both auditory and visual channels creates stronger memory traces. Color + sound together reinforce learning more than either channel alone.

Cognitive Load Theory

Learning is optimized when irrelevant mental effort is minimized. Color-coding offloads pattern recognition to the visual system, freeing your ears to focus on listening.

Stress-Free Learning

Pressure and anxiety impair analytical thinking. When you feel safe to explore without judgment, you listen more carefully and learn more deeply.
